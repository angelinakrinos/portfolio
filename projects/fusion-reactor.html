Conceptual Design of a Small-Scale Fusion Demonstration System

This document captures my initial engineering approach, calculations, and planning for building a compact, school-based fusion demonstration system. Although we haven’t started actual construction, the groundwork—detailed parameter studies, cost estimates, safety considerations, and regulatory inquiries— lays the foundation for eventually turning theory into reality.

1. Introduction & Objectives

The concept is straightforward on paper: use an inertial electrostatic confinement (IEC) approach to encourage deuterium ions to fuse, producing a measurable (though modest) neutron flux. The main objective is educational: I want to gain firsthand experience with high-voltage engineering, vacuum technology, and safe handling of low-level nuclear processes. The final setup should allow us to confirm neutron production, providing a tangible demonstration of fusion physics at a scale accessible to a high-school environment.

2. Fusion Principles & Intended Approach

The intended method is a Farnsworth-Hirsch fusor configuration. By creating a stable plasma within a vacuum chamber and applying a substantial DC voltage (tens of kV), deuterium ions are accelerated toward a central grid, where collisions can lead to fusion. While no net energy gain is expected, detecting the characteristic neutrons provides a clear signature of success.

Early theoretical estimates suggest that operating at around 20-50 kV and a few milliamps of current could yield a modest but detectable neutron count (~103–105/s). Verifying this experimentally is the core challenge.

3. Defining Operating Parameters

Before even considering equipment, I set some baseline parameters:

  • Voltage: 20–50 kV DC
  • Current: ~1–5 mA
  • Chamber Pressure: 10-4 to 10-5 Torr (requiring a decent vacuum system plus a fine deuterium feed)

At ~25 kV and ~5 mA, power consumption hovers around 125 W—manageable, but dangerous in terms of voltage level. A rough energy calculation guided me to confirm that such levels are common in amateur fusors yet demand strict insulation and grounding strategies.

4. High-Voltage Supply Challenges

The high-voltage supply is likely the hardest puzzle. Commercial lab-grade HV supplies (20-50 kV) can be expensive. On the other hand, makeshift setups from neon sign transformers and homemade multipliers are cheaper but less stable and riskier.

Current plan: Start by considering a used commercial HV supply for reliability and safety. This might cost $1,500-$2,500 alone. I’m also sketching out insulation requirements. At 30 kV, even a tiny void can trigger corona or arc-over. I need careful conductor geometry, oil or epoxy potting for high-voltage components, and a proper enclosure. Built-in current limiting and a fast trip circuit will reduce the likelihood of catastrophic failures.

5. Vacuum & Gas Handling

Achieving the desired pressure range likely means a two-stage approach: a rotary vane pump to get down to ~10-3 Torr, then a turbo or diffusion pump for the next two orders of magnitude. Used vacuum pumps and stainless steel chambers appear frequently on the secondhand market.

The chamber will probably be a small stainless steel sphere or cylinder (6–8 inch diameter), fitted with standard KF or CF flanges. I’ll incorporate a leak valve or mass flow controller for deuterium, targeting a stable partial pressure to maintain a glow discharge plasma. While the vacuum system isn’t cheap, careful shopping might keep it under $1,000.

6. Radiation Safety & Shielding

The fusion process will produce neutrons. Although at low rates, I need a shielding plan. Hydrogen-rich materials like polyethylene help moderate neutrons, and adding boron compounds (boric acid in water or borated polyethylene) captures them. A simple layered approach—HDPE blocks around the chamber and a boron-based absorber—should suffice. The aim is to keep any dose negligible.

I’m considering a small He-3 or BF3 neutron detector for verification. This instrumentation will confirm fusion events. It’s crucial that all radiation measurements comply with local guidelines. I will document and limit test run durations to ensure minimal exposure.

7. Regulatory & Compliance Considerations

Even though no fissile material is involved, I must consider the following:

  • School Administration Approval: Written permission from the school principal and science department.
  • Local Authorities: Possibly informing local health physics officials about the project. Ensuring compliance with local radiation guidelines is a priority.
  • Electrical Codes: High voltage demands adherence to electrical safety codes, proper grounding, and possibly external inspection.

I’ll finalize these steps before any hardware arrives, ensuring we have all clearances and safety directives in place.

8. Equipment Sourcing & Preliminary Budget

I’ve priced out a rough bill of materials:

  • HV Power Supply: ~$1,500–$2,500 (used, high-voltage DC supply)
  • Vacuum System (pumps, valves, chamber): ~$1,000–$1,500
  • Deuterium Gas & Flow Components: ~$300–$500
  • Neutron Detector & Shielding Materials: ~$500–$1,000

The total initial outlay might be $3,500–$5,500. This is not trivial for a school-based project, so grant applications, department funding, and possibly community sponsorship are on my radar.

9. Next Steps & Outlook

Before committing funds, my plan is:

  • Secure all administrative and safety approvals
  • Reach out to local universities or research labs for guidance and possibly borrowing some instruments
  • Order a basic vacuum pump and test vacuum integrity on a simple chamber mock-up
  • Refine HV supply selection based on safety and reliability, then purchase the main unit

If all goes well, the first plasma tests—without deuterium—could occur a few months after initial equipment arrival. Deuterium introduction and neutron detection would follow. The ultimate goal: a confirmable set of fusion runs, fully documented, safe, and hopefully inspiring future engineering projects at our school.
