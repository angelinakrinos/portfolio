SSI Europa Boat: Navigation Design for Exploration of Mars' Moons

This page outlines the early-stage engineering and design work for creating an autonomous, solar-powered boat, inspired by the robust capabilities of long-endurance ocean drones and the forward-looking challenge of navigating remote environments—like those on Mars' moons. While our end goal is ambitious, we’re currently taking practical, incremental steps: assembling hardware, integrating electronics, and testing scaled-down prototypes to guide us forward.

1. Introduction & Vision

Drawing inspiration from drone ships like Saildrone’s platforms and imagining what navigation might require on distant celestial oceans, this project aims to produce a robust, self-sufficient autonomous vessel. While we talk about “Europa” and Mars’ moons as a guiding theme, we’re firmly grounded on Earth: the immediate goal is a stable, solar-powered boat that can handle real-world oceanic conditions and collect environmental data autonomously.

2. Initial Concept & Materials

Our starting point: a 12 ft resin fishing boat sourced from Bass Pro Shops. Over this, we plan to mount an A-frame array of ten 100W solar panels. For propulsion, a 12V outboard motor (36 lbf thrust) and a 50 Ah marine battery will allow slow but continuous operation. The core idea is to test self-sufficiency and endurance on a modest scale—no custom hull design just yet, but a practical starting point.

3. Focus on Stability & Self-Righting

Ensuring the boat can survive harsh conditions is crucial. We plan to incorporate a keel weight below the hull, making the boat stable only when upright. If it capsizes, gravity and buoyancy should realign it. The A-frame solar panel structure and potentially adding foam inside the hull will help manage water ingress and prevent pooling, further enhancing stability.

4. Power & Propulsion Setup

With approximately 1 kW from the solar panels and a 640 W motor draw, we’re aiming for a balanced power budget. The boat might not be fast, but it should be steady and capable of running indefinitely under good sunlight conditions. Steering is handled by a 125 kgf servo directing the outboard motor’s angle.

On the electrical engineering (EE) side, I’ve contributed to selecting suitable wiring, ensuring correct voltage regulation, and planning for safe interconnects between the battery, motor controller, and servo drive. Balancing power flows and protecting components are key EE design concerns.

5. Navigation, Comms & Sensor Integration

We’re leveraging ArduPilot’s boat configuration on a Pixhawk flight controller paired with GPS for waypoint navigation. A Raspberry Pi 4 will log data (e.g., temperature, humidity, pressure) and possibly integrate a hydrophone for underwater acoustic data. We’ll use a RockBLOCK satellite modem to relay status updates back home.

My contributions on the systems side include integrating these electronics: mounting sensors, setting up the Pi for data acquisition, and configuring basic communication protocols. It’s still early, but we’ve done enough dry runs to confirm the Pi logs data reliably and the satellite link can send short messages.

6. Prototyping & Preliminary Tests

We’re not jumping straight into the ocean. Our first test platform is a smaller “bait boat” prototype—much more manageable and less risky for initial experiments. We’ve tested basic maneuvering in calm waters, verifying that the RC mode, motor control, and partial solar charging work as expected.

Next, we’ll graduate to the 12 ft resin boat, first doing loops in a local lake to test self-righting and waypoint navigation. After that, maybe the SF Bay for longer trials. Each step helps us refine mechanical setups, EE interfaces, and software code before tackling larger challenges.

7. Long-Term Outlook

Ultimately, we imagine pushing the boat to travel farther offshore, maybe even attempt reaching places like Point Nemo. The lessons learned in incremental testing—power budgeting, communication resilience, navigation reliability—could, in theory, inform how we might design exploratory craft for alien oceans someday.

8. Conclusion

Right now, this project is still in a design-focused, scrappy, early-prototyping stage. We’re piecing together off-the-shelf parts, testing our first systems on a small scale, and learning as we go. My role in both EE design and general systems integration ensures that as we progress, we stay grounded in practicality while keeping our sights on a big-picture vision.
