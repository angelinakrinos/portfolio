EE101B Lab 5: Class D Audio Amplifier

This report describes building and characterizing a class-D audio amplifier. From selecting components to refining our soldering techniques, the project merged theoretical circuit concepts with hands-on fabrication, ultimately producing a functional, efficient amplifier tested with real-world signals.

1. Introduction & Objectives

Class-D amplifiers offer high efficiency by using switching transistors and LC filters to reconstruct audio signals. Our goal: implement a TI TPA3122D2-based amplifier from scratch, verifying that the output waveform matches expectations and that distortion remains low.

The objective was to learn how switching topologies differ from linear amps, and how component choices and layout affect sound quality and efficiency.

2. Circuit Design & Component Selection

We followed a reference design, incorporating decoupling capacitors, LC output filters, and biasing resistors to set the gain. Electrolytic capacitors handled power supply filtering; ceramic capacitors ensured stable high-frequency operation.

Our chosen inductors were slightly higher than the reference (47μH vs. 22μH), which still provided adequate filtering. Potentiometers were replaced with fixed resistors for maximum gain settings. All design decisions aimed at stable, low-distortion audio output.

3. Assembly & Soldering Process

Following best soldering practices, we placed components close to the IC, ensuring short paths for critical signals. We tack-soldered first, checked VCC and GND continuity, then proceeded with final solder beads. Careful attention to polarity on electrolytics was critical—reversing them can cause failures or explosions.

After assembly, we inspected joints for cold solder and tidied up connections. A stable mechanical build meant less noise pickup and more consistent results.

4. Measurements & Performance

With VCC at 12V, idle current around 20 mA confirmed low quiescent power consumption. Under signal input, the amplifier reproduced 1 kHz tones with a peak-to-peak voltage ~6.4V at the load. We measured harmonic components and found THD around 0.041, indicating decent audio fidelity.

Sweeping input frequencies (10 Hz to 50 kHz) showed the LC filters effectively removing unwanted high-frequency components, ensuring the output resembled a clean analog waveform from a switching system.

5. Key Lessons Learned

  • Soldering & Layout Matters: Physical layout heavily influences noise and stability.
  • Power & Efficiency: Higher voltages increased efficiency, demonstrating why class-D amps are preferred in portable applications.
  • Testing Strategy: Systematic measurements (THD, frequency response) provided clear benchmarks for improvement.

6. Future Refinements

Potential improvements include implementing a closed-loop feedback path for even lower distortion, adding better shielding against EMI, or testing different inductor values for optimal filter response. We could also integrate a Bluetooth module for wireless audio input.

7. Conclusion

Building and testing the class-D amplifier deepened my understanding of switching power circuits, practical layout constraints, and the importance of careful component selection. The final amplifier worked reliably, offering a hands-on demonstration of how class-D principles translate into real, efficient audio systems.
